Direction for the Field of a Point Charge

A point charge creates an electric field that points directly away from or toward itself. A formula to compute the magnitude of the field was provided in the last section. Here we will consider how to determine the (Cartesian) components of the field vector.

The trick is that the displacement vector, starting at the charge and ending at the "field point" has the same direction as the field vector. Therefore, the right triangles formed by the components of these vectors are similar. This means that they as the same shape, just scaled differently.

The implications of this geometric fact are that the ratios of the side lengths of these two triangle are equal. \[ \frac{E_x}{E} = \frac{r_x}{r} \] \[ \frac{E_y}{E} = \frac{r_y}{r} \]

The equations provided above are sufficient for computing the electric field vector, if you have the components of the displacement vector and the field magnitude. However, we can combine these equations into a single vector equation that nicely encapsulates the relationship between the field this displacement vector. \[ \vec{E} = E_x \hat{\imath} + E_y \hat{\jmath} = E \left( \frac{r_x \hat{\imath} + r_y \hat{\jmath}}{r} \right) = E \, \hat{r} \]

In the final expression, the symbol \(\hat{r}\) is the direction vector of the displacement vector. Thus, this equation nicely shows the electric field of a point charge can be broken into two parts: its magnitude and its direction, which is the same as the direction of the displacement vector.
